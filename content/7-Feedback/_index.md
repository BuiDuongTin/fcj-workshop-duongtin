This section summarizes what I took away from the First Cloud Journey internship and the HaShop project report. The experience did more than teach me AWS topics; it also showed me how to turn technical ideas into something structured, explainable, and ready to validate.

### 1. What I Learned Most

The biggest value for me was the way the program was paced. Each week focused on a different piece of AWS, starting with fundamentals like IAM, VPC, EC2, and RDS and then moving toward more operational concerns such as logging, monitoring, cost awareness, security, automation, and clean-up. That rhythm helped AWS feel less like a list of separate services and more like parts of one system.

It also changed the way I read documentation. Instead of focusing only on features, I started asking what problem a service solves, how it affects the flow, and whether it is truly necessary in the architecture.

### 2. Moving Into the Project

Once I started working on HaShop, I realized the challenge was not simply deploying individual services. The real task was connecting them into a clear story: how users move through the system, how requests are handled, where authentication happens, how back-end components exchange data, and where synchronous or asynchronous design makes more sense.

That process taught me that a cloud project is never just about getting something to run. It also needs design reasoning, security awareness, observability, testing, and a proper end-of-life plan so resources do not stay active longer than necessary. That made the report feel more meaningful than a simple technology summary.

### 3. What Helped the Most

The materials and labs were a big part of why I could keep moving forward. The lessons were broken into manageable steps, so I never had to absorb everything at once. That made it easier to take notes, review the content, and turn those notes into a 12-week worklog without losing the thread.

Feedback during the report-writing stage also helped me rethink the structure. I refined the flow of the content, added deployment and testing details, and made the English and Vietnamese versions line up more closely in both structure and intent.

### 4. Changes in My Own Skills

After completing the internship report, I improved in the following areas:

- I became more confident when reading AWS documentation and extracting only what I needed.
- I got better at turning scattered notes into content with a clear flow.
- I started looking at an architecture from multiple angles instead of focusing only on deployment.
- I paid more attention to presentation, because technical writing needs to be consistent and easy to follow.
- I understood more clearly that testing, monitoring, and clean-up matter as much as building the system itself.
- I became more comfortable writing bilingual content while keeping the core message aligned.

### 5. What Could Be Improved

If the program provided more real examples earlier, it would be easier for learners to understand the expected output. A few sample reports, architecture diagrams, or deployment write-ups would reduce the amount of guesswork later in the process.

It would also help to have a clearer evaluation framework from the beginning. That way, students would know whether to focus more on architecture, service choices, request flow, testing, monitoring, or the final summary. The project would feel less open-ended and more guided.

### 6. My Closing Reflection

For me, First Cloud Journey was valuable because it taught not only tools but also the mindset needed when working with cloud systems. By the end of the program, I felt more prepared to read documentation, design solutions, and explain technical ideas more clearly.

If I had more time, I would continue improving the project by making deployment automation, system monitoring, and failure testing more complete so I could understand the behavior of a cloud system in greater depth.
